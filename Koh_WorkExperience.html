Work Experience

My work experience over the past few years has consisted mostly of field trips, lectures, and summer programs. One notable experience was my field trip to the partnership summit at SAP, where I had the opportunity to shadow an account executive. This experience was useful because it enabled me to observe how SAP used their representatives to interact with and make deals with representatives from other corporations. The importance of these Inter-corporate relationships apply to all companies, regardless of their size. Another memorable experience was my tour of the National Ignition Facility (NIF) at Lawrence Livermore National Laboratory (LLNL). Here, I was fortunate to meet a laser Scientist who showed us around and explained how the facility operated. The engineers there worked in a huge facility, and thus had to ensure that the facility was maintained, operational, and safe. This experience was useful because it allowed me to realize that facilities such as this one can be used to advance the fields of science, medicine, and technology, and thus, progress stems from engineering.

On February 17th, 2017, I attended a lecture by Felicie Albert, an LLNL scientist. She introduced particle accelerators and explained their advantages, disadvantages, limitations, and capabilities. Then, she revealed a project that her team had been working on - a laser wakefield accelerator. This construction was designed to perform the same tasks as particle accelerators, while reducing the space occupied. This was an inspiring experience for me because the solution she presented was a direct application of the design process in the engineering field. This is something that I, as an aspiring engineer, aim to practice in my projects and in my work. During my time at COSMOS, a four-week summer program, I had the opportunity to attend Professor Raymond de Callafon's lectures. Professor de Callafon is involved in the department Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering, so his talks and lessons were of great interest to me. Professor De Callafon took great care in explaining the physics of a ball drop from a ramp, which turned out to be much more complicated than I expected. However, I managed to understand and extract the most relevant parts of the talks, and apply them to my final project. I was inspired by Professor De Callafon's knowledge and teaching skill, and I aim to be as effective of a leader and mentor as he was.

I have been very fortunate to have Jason Franco as my mentor as part of our school's engineering mentorship program. Mr. Franco is president of his own startup, JVF consulting, which has found success in the fourteen years that it has been in existence. However, despite his successes, Mr. Franco has taught me that what success is is not set in stone; it is defined by the person who works for it. In other words, if I want to be successful, I first need to determine what success really looks like. After much discussion, we decided that an important aspect of success was interpersonal interactions - that is, relationships with other people. In addition to teaching me about success, Mr. Franco also gave me feedback on my resume and engineering portfolio. I learned that the information in my resume and portfolio has to be concise and appealing. When writing a resume, one should put themselves into the position of an employer and determine if the resume looks appealing to them. This practice applies not only to resumes but also to applications for positions or colleges.

In my 8th grade year, I helped to found an organization called Fallon Engineering Lab with the goal to teach middle school students the basic principles of engineering and programming. There, I was able to act as a mentor to those students, and helped them understand the concepts involved in working with a Raspberry Pi. Since I already had experience working with Raspberry Pis, I volunteered to build and program a microcontroller-enabled robot for the class. My prototype ended up being the model robot for the students to attempt to build. Since I was the one who built the robot, I was able to help the students in a useful and effective way. This experience in mentorship also helped to prepare me for my current officer position in our school's robotics club, in which I have to utilize the skills that I have learned from my time volunteering in Fallon Engineering Lab.
